A working list of principles for how AI is used on this site and in the work behind it. Short list, expected to grow.

## 1. Be clear about who's speaking

Anything on this site shown in regular type was written by Nico, or by someone Nico read and edited carefully enough to vouch for. Anything written by a language model — Claude, mostly — appears in muted italic with a marker above it: ↳

This applies to:

- per-project `evolution` rollups (auto-generated at the end of each session)
- the "from claude" callouts on the [about](/about) page and elsewhere
- anything else that emerges from the agentic workflow without a deliberate human authoring step

The goal isn't to flag AI work as lesser. It's so a reader always knows whose voice they're reading.

## 2. Build in the open

The code behind these projects is public unless there's a real reason it can't be. You learn how something works by reading it, so the work stays readable — this site included. Open-source is the default; anything closed should be the exception that earns it.

## 3. Make it pro-social

The test for anything built here: does it leave people better off — more connected, more capable, more curious? The tools are meant to bring people together, teach, or make something. Not to capture attention, farm engagement, or harvest data. If a feature only wins by taking from the person using it, it doesn't ship.

## 4. Civility by design, not by policing

When people talk here, the easy, default path should be the decent one — built into how the space works rather than bolted on after. Real names over anonymity, slower exchanges over hot takes, small rooms over crowds. The aim is to make being thoughtful the path of least resistance, instead of moderating away the damage later.
